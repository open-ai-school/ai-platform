## What We'll Build

We're going to train an AI that recognises drawings. You'll:

1. **Draw examples** of different shapes (circle, square, triangle)
2. **Label them** ("this is a circle", "this is a square")
3. **Watch the AI learn** from your examples
4. **Test it** with new drawings to see if it learned correctly

This is real machine learning — the same concept used by Google, Apple, and every major tech company.

## What's Happening Behind the Scenes?

When you draw and the AI guesses, here's what's happening in simple terms:

1. **Your drawing becomes numbers** — The computer doesn't "see" your drawing. It converts it into a grid of numbers (bright pixels = high numbers, dark pixels = low numbers)

2. **It compares to known patterns** — The AI has been trained on thousands of similar drawings. It knows that circles tend to have certain patterns of numbers, squares have others

3. **It calculates confidence** — For each shape category, it calculates a percentage: "72% circle, 18% oval, 10% square"

4. **It gives its best guess** — The highest percentage wins!

> This is the same basic process used in medical imaging — doctors' AI tools look at X-rays and compare them to millions of previous X-rays to detect problems.

## Understanding the Results

When you see the AI's guess:

<Illustration src="/images/lessons/confidence-meter.svg" alt="Confidence levels: High (80%+), Medium (50-80%), Low (below 50%)" caption="The higher the confidence, the more the AI's prediction matches its training data" />

- **High confidence (80%+)** — The AI is quite sure. Your drawing closely matches patterns it has seen before.
- **Medium confidence (50-80%)** — The AI is somewhat sure, but the drawing could match multiple shapes.
- **Low confidence (below 50%)** — The AI is guessing. Your drawing doesn't clearly match any pattern it knows.

<ThinkAboutIt>
What happens when you draw something the AI has never seen — like a house or a cat? It will still try to classify it as one of its known shapes. This is a limitation: AI can only recognise what it's been trained on.
</ThinkAboutIt>

## Experiment Ideas

Try these experiments to understand AI better:

### Experiment 1: Draw the Same Shape Differently
Draw a circle 5 different ways — big, small, thick, thin, wobbly. Does the AI still recognise all of them?

### Experiment 2: Try to Trick It
Draw something between a circle and a square (a rounded square). What does the AI say? This shows you how AI handles ambiguity.

### Experiment 3: Draw Something Unknown
Draw a star or a heart. The AI wasn't trained on these — what happens? This demonstrates the limits of training data.

## What You've Just Done

Congratulations! 🎉 You've just experienced the core concepts of AI:

- **Training data** — The examples the AI learned from
- **Pattern recognition** — How the AI identifies shapes
- **Prediction** — Making a guess based on learned patterns
- **Confidence scores** — How certain the AI is
- **Limitations** — AI can only recognise what it was trained on

These same concepts power everything from self-driving cars to medical diagnosis to voice assistants.

## Quick Recap 🎯

1. AI converts your drawing into numbers and compares it to known patterns
2. Higher confidence = the drawing more closely matches known examples
3. AI can only recognise what it's been trained on — it can't handle truly unknown things
4. The same basic process powers real-world AI applications
